19 vital parts for your website page to maximize user engagement

10 Jan

SCROLL

Chances are, there’s been a time when you’ve clicked through endless links, scrolling page after page in a seemingly futile quest for the right information.

Valentine Boyev

CEO at Halo Lab

Web design and page structure play a big role in encouraging users to click and scroll.

Why do you need to think about page structure

Let’s start by defining what a webpage is, as it is not the same as a website.

The organized webpage is the foundation of your online presence.

A well-structured page leads users smoothly from beginning to end.

19 crucial elements of a web page

When it comes to designing a webpage, it’s all about picking the right elements.

1. Header

First up is the header or a site menu.

The header is your first chance to make an impression, so you’ve got to make it count.

Your header is a site guide that ensures visitors know exactly where they are and how to find what they’re looking for.

A visible and informative header presents a site’s content

Uncover the secrets of impactful header design through our best practices guide in the Website Header Inspiration article.

2. CTA button

Next on the list is the Call to Action (CTA) buttons that guide your visitors on what to do next, whether it’s “Buy Now,” “Learn More,” or “Sign Up.”

However, it’s not just about slapping a button anywhere.

Remember, the CTA button is your chance to convert a casual visitor into a customer or subscriber.

A clear CTA button leads users to the next step

3. Hero section

Moving on, let’s talk about the hero section.

Whether you’re showcasing a product, highlighting a service, or setting the mood, your hero section should be visually striking and emotionally resonant.

This component needs to connect with your visitors immediately, telling them what your site is about and what they can expect from it.

A hero section captivates at first glance

4. Footer

Scrolling down to the bottom of the page, you’ll find the footer.

This section often contains important links, contact information, social media icons, and sometimes a brief about the company or a sign-up form for newsletters.

This is the last impression you leave on your visitors, so make it a good one, reflecting the professionalism and comprehensiveness of your site.

Structured info in a footer does not overload the user

To enhance your understanding, explore the example for using footers in our feature article on Website Footer Design.

5. Slider

Up next is the slider.

Whether it’s the latest offers, featured products, or key messages, the slider lets you present content in an engaging, easily digestible format.

Compactly presented media via slider saves space on a page

6. Search

Another useful component is a search function tucked away in the header or an easily accessible place.

Whether your site is packed with loads of media or a wide range of products, a search field helps cut through the clutter.

A search field greatly improves website navigation

To explore the best ways to shape a research page, be sure to read our Search Results Page Design article.

7. Menu and navigation

When we think about easy navigation across a webpage, the menu is the main element that comes to mind.

There are various menu types to choose from, each serving different design needs and user experiences.

For sites with a wide range of topics or products, the mega menu provides a comprehensive view of all options in one large panel.

An interactive menu visually attracts the user

8. Breadcrumbs

Next in line is breadcrumbs. No, we’re not talking about the kind you find in the kitchen, but the digital steps that help guide visitors through your website.

The success is in the details, and nowhere is this truer than in the world of web design.

Typically appearing at the top of a page, this navigational feature shows users their current location on your site and how they got there.

Breadcrumbs are a visual guide for users

9. Forms

Whether it’s for signing up for a newsletter, making a purchase, or getting in touch, forms are the go-to method for visitors to interact and communicate with your site.

It should ask for only the necessary information, making the process quick and hassle-free.

A form improves interactions between your website and its users

10. Cards

Move on to the next element — cards.

A card system visually improves the content structure

Gain insights into card-based style by investigating cases and tips showcased in the Card UI Design article.

11. Videos

Media materials are a big deal. When was the last time you scrolled past a video without at least a curious glance?

So, why not harness the power of video on your website?

Videos make users stay on a website longer

12. Progress indicator

Have you ever filled out a form or completed a task online and wondered, “How much longer is this going to take?”

Think of it as the digital equivalent of breadcrumbs leading to the finish line

But why stop at just being functional? Get creative with it! Use colors, animations, or even fun little messages that pop up as the user progresses.

Progress indicator let users know at what stage they are now

13. Favicon

There is another small yet mighty tool — favicon. That is a tiny icon in your browser tab next to a website’s name.

A favicon is your website’s mini calling card that makes a big impression.

Creating a favicon is not just about shrinking your logo into a tiny square.

A memorable favicon improves brand awareness

14. Tags

Another versatile element on your webpage is tags.

They’re also a big win for SEO.

Strategically placed tags enhance website usability

15. Links

Links are your way of guiding visitors around your site and even beyond it.

You probably clicked on a link, thinking it would take you somewhere, and ended up in a completely unexpected place.

Easy-to-spot links are more likely to be clicked on

16. Quality images

Let’s shine a spotlight on quality images.

Check whether each image is in line with your identity, as the wrong visuals can lead to confusion among users.

Explore the art of image optimization and get the Image Resizing Halo Lab plugin in the article How to Optimize Images.

And remember, diversity and authenticity in your imagery can make your website feel more inclusive and relatable.

Quality images should tie in with your brand message

17. Typography

We’re close to the finish line, and the next element is typography.

Then there’s readability. Think about font size, color contrast, and spacing to make text readable.

If you want to learn more about fonts, read our article "24 serif font examples to improve your branding and website design in 2024."

And let’s not forget about special fonts for headings and accents — they’re like the highlighters of your text, drawing attention to key areas.

Typography should be readable regardless of the background

18. White space

Time to talk about white space, often the invisible element of great web design.

It doesn’t necessarily have to be white, by the way.

And balance is critical. Too little white space can turn your site into a cramped one, but too much might make it seem empty or unfinished.

Strategically used white space complements a site’s aesthetic

19. Sidebar

Finally, a sidebar is a trusty sidekick to your website’s main content with extra information or functionality.

A well-crafted sidebar is like a helpful guide, always offering something more.

A well-designed sidebar should be a natural extension of a website tailored to fit the content of your page.

A sidebar provides users with additional navigation

Final point

And there we have it — a comprehensive journey through the essential elements of a webpage, from top to bottom.

So, as you begin your web design journey, keep these elements in mind.
